## Post 13: The Hiring Decision You Made Without Data
**Date:** Monday, Mar 9

---

You approved a new hire last month.

Without a financial model.

I've done it too. More times than I'd like to admit.

Revenue is up. Team is stretched. Someone makes a case for headcount. I say yes.

Never checked whether the burn rate could absorb it for 12 months.
Never modeled whether the role pays for itself in 2 quarters.

Not a judgment issue. A speed issue.

Running that analysis meant waiting 3 days. I wasn't going to slow down a hiring decision for 3 days.

So I made the biggest expense decision in my company on instinct.

I spent $180K on a hire I approved in one meeting. The vendor contract I agonized over for a week? $12K.

Headcount is the largest line on most P&Ls. And most CEOs approve it faster than any other expense.

Quick check: For your last 3 hires, can you show the financial model that justified each one?

If the answer is "there wasn't one" — that's the problem.

## Post 14: The Revenue Number That's Lying to You
**Date:** Wednesday, Mar 11

---

Revenue is up 40%.

Congratulations.

Now tell me: which of your customers are actually profitable?

I celebrated a revenue milestone once. Then my CFO cornered me with a spreadsheet I didn't want to see.

Our top client by revenue was our most expensive client to serve.

Custom work. Endless revisions. We never said no because the invoice was big.

We were funding their account with profits from customers who paid us fairly.

The scary part isn't that I missed it once.

It's that this went on for over a year. Margins shrank while revenue grew. The board saw the top line and said "great job."

The bleeding continued. Quietly.

Rank your top 10 customers by net margin, not revenue.

If you can't do that without a multi-day effort, ask yourself how long your most expensive customers have been hiding behind your best ones.

That's the trap growing companies don't see until it's too late.

## Post 15: The Cash Conversation You're Avoiding
**Date:** Friday, Mar 13

---

Every CEO knows their bank balance.

Almost none know their cash position.

These are not the same thing.

Bank balance is a screenshot. Cash position is a movie — what's coming in, what's going out, and whether you make payroll in 90 days.

I checked my bank balance every morning. It felt responsible.

It wasn't. Because I wasn't factoring in:
→ $120K in receivables 45+ days overdue
→ A quarterly tax payment due in 6 weeks
→ Vendor contracts renewing at higher rates
→ Deferred revenue that wasn't mine to spend

My bank balance said "healthy."
My cash position said "you have a problem in 60 days."

I never connected those dots. I checked the balance every morning and told myself I was managing cash.

I wasn't managing anything. I was guessing.

Can you tell me right now your projected cash position 90 days out, accounting for all committed obligations?

If you can't, you're not managing cash.

You're guessing. And hoping the guess is right.

---

**Visual:** Two side-by-side screens. Left: "Bank Balance: $842,000" with a green checkmark. Right: "Cash Position (90 days): -$31,000" with a red warning. Title: "Which one are you looking at?"

**First comment:** "Cash forecasting from ERP data should be continuous, not quarterly. Your ERP records every transaction in real time — the problem is nobody's synthesizing it into a forward-looking view. That's what we built."
