## Post 7: The "Stale Data" Problem
**Date:** Monday, Feb 24
**Topic:** Governing on outdated financial data
**Hook angle:** A specific, uncomfortable realization every CEO has had

---

Your board deck is 30 days old the moment you present it.

Think about that.

You walk into the board meeting with Q4 numbers.
The board asks about January trends.
You say "we're still closing January."

That's not a finance team failure.
That's a structural problem with how financial data works at most companies.

Here's what I realized running my own company:

Every decision I make between closes is based on last month's numbers.

Hiring decisions → based on last month's burn.
Pricing decisions → based on last month's margins.
Investment decisions → based on last month's cash position.

But the business moved since last month.
Expenses shifted. Revenue recognized differently. A big invoice came in — or didn't.

I was governing on memory. Not data.

And the worst part?

The real-time data existed.
It was sitting in my ERP.
Every transaction, every invoice, every payment — recorded the moment it happened.

I just couldn't get to it without asking someone to pull it.
And that takes 2-3 days.

So I stopped asking.
And started making decisions on instinct instead.

That's the moment I knew something was fundamentally broken.

Not in my finance team. In the system.

Here's a quick check for yourself:

When was the last time you asked a financial question about your business and got the answer the same day?

If you can't remember, you're not running on data.
You're running on the last snapshot someone gave you.

And that snapshot is already out of date.

## Post 8: The "Three Numbers" Test
**Date:** Wednesday, Feb 26
**Topic:** CEOs who can't answer basic financial questions about their own business
**Hook angle:** An interactive challenge that makes the problem personal

---

Quick test.

Without checking anything — no dashboards, no texts, no emails — answer these three questions about your business right now:

1. What is your burn rate this month?
2. Who is your largest outstanding receivable?
3. What is your fastest-growing expense category?

Done?

If you answered all three from memory with confidence → you're in the top 5% of CEOs I've met.

If you got one or two → you're normal. And that should concern you.

If you opened a new tab to check → that's the problem I'm talking about.

Here's what bothers me about this:

These aren't obscure metrics.
They're the three most basic financial questions about running a business.

And most CEOs — myself included, until recently — can't answer them without asking someone.

That "someone" is usually a CFO or Controller who needs 1-3 days to pull the data.

So the CEO makes the decision anyway. Without the numbers.

I did it for years.

Approved hires based on "revenue is trending up" instead of checking if the burn rate could absorb it for 12 months.

Ignored receivables because "collections are handled" — until one client owed us 40% of our monthly revenue and was 60 days late.

Let an expense line grow 35% quarter-over-quarter because nobody flagged it until the board asked.

Every one of those was a data problem.
Not a judgment problem.

The data existed.
I just couldn't get to it fast enough to use it.

The goal isn't more reports.
The goal is that a CEO can answer the 3 most basic questions about their business at any moment.

Without waiting. Without asking. Without guessing.

How did you score on the test? Be honest. I'll go first — 6 months ago, I would've failed all three.

## Post 9: The Board Meeting You're Not Prepared For
**Date:** Friday, Feb 28
**Topic:** The moment a board asks a question the CEO can't answer
**Hook angle:** A specific, high-stakes moment every funded CEO dreads

---

There is a moment in every board meeting that separates CEOs who know their numbers from CEOs who think they know their numbers.

It's not the revenue slide.
It's not the pipeline update.

It's the follow-up question.

"What's your customer acquisition cost by channel?"
"What's the revenue concentration in your top 3 accounts?"
"What does your cash conversion cycle look like this quarter vs. last?"

These aren't gotcha questions.
They're the questions a good board is supposed to ask.

And most CEOs — including me, at one point — respond with some version of:

"Great question. Let me get back to you on that."

That sentence costs you more than you think.

It tells the board you don't have real-time access to your own financial data.
It tells them the next set of numbers you present might also be approximate.
It creates a small crack in confidence. And cracks compound.

Here's what I learned:

The CEOs who impress their boards aren't the ones with better data.
They're the ones with faster access to the same data.

Their ERP has the same information yours does.
The difference is they can get to it before the board meeting, not after.

I started preparing differently.

Before every board meeting, I now make a list of the 10 most uncomfortable questions the board could ask.

Then I check: can I answer each one right now, from my existing data?

The ones I can't answer aren't knowledge gaps.
They're access gaps.

The data is in the ERP.
I just need a faster way to get to it.

Try this before your next board meeting:

Write down the 5 questions your board is most likely to ask.
Try to answer them using only what you know right now.

If you can't answer 3 out of 5 without emailing your finance team, you have a preparation problem.

And it's not a problem more prep time will solve.
It's a problem better data access will solve.

What's the one board question you dread most? I'll share mine if you share yours.
