## Post 10: Your CFO Should Be Your Strategist, Not Your Data Analyst
**Date:** Monday, Mar 2

---

Your CFO didn't spend 15 years in finance to export CSVs for you.

But that's what happens every time you ask "What's our burn rate this month?"

You ask the question.
They open the ERP.
Export. Clean. Format. Send.

2 days later, you get your answer.
Your CFO lost 2 days.

I realized I'd turned my CFO into a human API between me and our ERP.

That wasn't their failure. It was mine.

Every hour they spend pulling data is an hour they're not spending on:
→ Flagging a cash problem before it becomes a crisis
→ Modeling the impact of a pricing change
→ Telling me "don't do that, here's why"

The best CEO-CFO partnerships have one thing in common:

The CEO gives the CFO leverage.

Not more headcount. Not more tools.
Leverage that removes the extraction work entirely.

Ask your CFO this week: "What percentage of your time goes to pulling data vs. analyzing it?"

The answer will be uncomfortable for both of you.

## Post 11: The "Finance Tax" on Every Decision
**Date:** Wednesday, Mar 4

---

Every strategic decision in your company has a hidden tax.

I call it the Finance Tax.

Can you afford to hire two engineers? → 3-day question.
Should you renew that vendor contract? → 3-day question.
What's your runway at current burn? → 3-day question.

Your CFO probably knows the answer intuitively.

But gut feel doesn't go in a board deck.

So they pull the data. Build the model. Validate.
3 days later, you get the answer.

By then, you've already decided.

I'd ask a question on Monday.
By Wednesday, I'd made the call.
The analysis arrived Thursday.

I wasn't ignoring my CFO.
I just couldn't wait.

The Finance Tax doesn't show up in your P&L.
But it shows up in every decision made without data — because the data took too long.

Quick test: List your last 5 strategic decisions.

How many had the financial data before the decision — and how many got it after?

If "after" wins, the Finance Tax is costing you more than you think.

## Post 12: What Your ERP Already Knows (That Neither of You Can Access)
**Date:** Friday, Mar 6

---

Your ERP already knows your customer concentration risk.
It knows which expense line grew 35% last quarter.
It knows which vendor you're overpaying.

Your CFO probably suspects all of this.

But proving it requires pulling 6 reports, cleaning the data, and spending a week on analysis that should take 30 seconds.

So nobody asks.

Not because they don't care.
Because asking is too expensive.

I made a list of questions I wished I could ask my ERP:

→ Which customers are more than 15% of my revenue?
→ What's the trend on my top 5 expense categories?
→ Which invoices over $10K are 45+ days outstanding?

Every one of these can be answered with data already in my ERP.

Not one of them can be answered by my ERP.

The data is there. The access isn't.

Then I tried something:

I asked my CFO: "If you had 10 extra hours this week — no data pulling — what would you tell me about our business?"

They came back with 8 things.

Eight insights they were sitting on but couldn't prove because the extraction work would take a month.

Try it with yours.

The gap between what your CFO knows and what they can show you — that's the gap worth closing.
