# Larry Launch Content Style Guide v2
## Updated for CEO/Decision-Maker Positioning (Posts 7-27)

This document captures the revised preferences, constraints, and tonal guidelines for the remaining 7 weeks of the Larry launch campaign (Posts 7-27).
**Use this file as a prompt prefix when drafting all future posts.**

## 1. Core Persona & Voice

### The Persona: "The CEO Who Built the Answer"
- **DO write as:** A CEO who lived the problem of not being able to access his own financial data. Speaking from firsthand experience running a company, not from consulting or observing others.
- **DO NOT write as:** A consultant diagnosing other people's processes. An AI vendor pitching a product. A thought leader dispensing generic advice.

### The Tone:
- **First-person authority:** "I couldn't answer this question about my own business" — not "I see CEOs struggling with this."
- **Specific, not abstract:** Name the exact question, the exact delay, the exact dollar amount.
- **Conversational urgency:** Short sentences. Real frustration. No corporate polish.
- **Challenging, not preachy:** "Can you answer this right now?" — puts the reader in the hot seat without lecturing them.

### Voice Patterns:
- "I asked my team [specific question]. It took [specific time]."
- "Quick test: Can you tell me [specific financial metric] right now without checking?"
- "The answer was in my ERP the whole time. I just couldn't get to it."
- "This isn't a finance team problem. It's a data access problem."
- "My CFO is brilliant. But I was making them spend 70% of their time pulling data instead of advising me."

### Critical Constraint — CEO-CFO Relationship:
- **NEVER position the CFO as the problem.** The CFO is the CEO's strategic partner, trapped by bad tooling.
- The enemy is the manual data extraction layer — not the people.
- Frame the CEO's role as *enabling* the CFO: "My job is to give my CFO leverage, not more data requests."
- When discussing the CEO-CFO information gap, the tone is empathy and shared frustration — never blame.

## 2. Target Audience

### Primary:
- **CEOs** (especially founder-CEOs at Series A-C companies who are still close to the financial decisions)
- **CFOs who think like CEOs** (strategic, decision-speed-oriented, not just compliance-focused)

### Secondary:
- Finance Directors, VPs of Finance who report to the CEO and feel the pressure of slow answers

### Explicitly NOT targeting:
- Controllers (they may read and benefit, but the content doesn't speak to their daily operational work)
- Accountants or bookkeepers
- Generic "Founders" or "Startups" — focus on leaders of growing companies with ERP data

## 3. Launch Strategy & "The Reveal"

### Phase 2 (Weeks 3-6, Posts 7-18): "The CEO's Data Problem"
- **Constraint: NEVER mention "Larry" by name.** Continue using "Komplai" or describe the concept.
- **Focus:** Sell the *problem* (CEOs can't access their own financial data fast enough) and tease the *paradigm shift* (what if you could just ask your ERP questions?).
- **Key narrative arc:** Stale data → CFO bottleneck → blind decisions → the intelligence layer idea

### Phase 3 (Weeks 7-9, Posts 19-27): "Meet the Intelligence Layer"
- **Action:** Introduce "Larry" by name as the answer to everything discussed in Phases 1-2.
- **Framing:** Larry is the intelligence layer for your ERP. Not a finance tool. Not a close manager. An intelligence layer.

## 4. Content Format: "The CEO Challenge"

Every post must include a specific, personal challenge the reader can try immediately:

- **The Quick Test:** "Can you answer [specific financial question] right now, without asking anyone?"
- **The Time Check:** "How long does it take to get [this specific answer] from your team?"
- **The Blind Spot:** "Do you know [this specific metric]? If not, here's what you're missing."
- **The Scenario:** "What would you do if [specific situation]? Do you have the data to decide?"

**The shift from v1:** Posts 1-6 used "run this audit on your team" (operator framing). Posts 7-27 use "can YOU answer this about YOUR business" (decision-maker framing).

## 5. Key Themes & Vocabulary

### Problems (CEO language):
- **Decision delay:** "3-day answer," "finance tax," "waiting for data"
- **Blind spots:** "Flying blind," "governing on stale data," "between closes"
- **Access gap:** "The data exists, you just can't get to it," "ERP is a filing cabinet"
- **Trust gap:** "Are these numbers current?," "board confidence," "I think vs. I know"

### Solutions (CEO language):
- **Speed:** "30-second answer," "same-day visibility," "time-to-answer"
- **Access:** "Talk to your data," "ask your ERP," "conversational finance"
- **Intelligence:** "Your ERP as an advisor," "proactive insights," "the intelligence layer"
- **Control:** "Know your business in real time," "stop waiting, start asking"

### Vocabulary to AVOID:
- Reconciliation, accruals, journal entries, close cycle (Controller language)
- "AI-powered," "next-gen," "cutting-edge" (generic tech language)
- "Streamline," "optimize," "efficiency" (consultant language)
- "Platform," "solution," "tool" (vendor language — use "intelligence layer" or just "Larry")

## 6. Formatting Rules

- **Headlines:** Must be a specific, challenging statement — not a generic observation.
- **Structure:** Hook (the CEO's pain) → Story (specific example or personal experience) → The Challenge (can you answer this?) → The Shift (what it should look like) → CTA
- **Visuals:** Minimal. When used, should illustrate a gap (decision made vs. data available) or a contrast (3 days vs. 30 seconds).
- **No markdown bold, headers, or tables in the actual post** — LinkedIn doesn't render them.
- **One thought per line** for mobile readability.
- **Hook in first 2-3 lines** (above the "...see more" fold).
- **Posts between 800-1,200 characters.** This is a hard ceiling. Cut ruthlessly. One idea per post.
- **No external links in post body** — put in first comment.
- **CTA asks the reader to try something** — not just share an opinion.
